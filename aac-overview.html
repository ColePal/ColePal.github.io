Recap: AAC from the Introduction to Communication Module

💬 What is AAC?

AAC stands for Augmentative and Alternative Communication. It includes all the ways students communicate that are different to or add onto speaking.

Augmentative = adding onto speech  |  Alternative = instead of speech

We use AAC to help students understand us, as well as to support them to express themselves. AAC comes in three forms:

🟢 Low-tech

Coreboards, PODD books, visual schedules, yes/no lanyards, single visual symbols

🟡 Mid-tech

Simple devices with recorded messages — e.g. buttons or switches that say "I need a break" or "I want more"

🔵 High-tech

Tablets or iPads with communication apps. Students access vocabulary by touch, using switches, or eye gaze.

🌟 Why do we use AAC?

  • Many students cannot rely on speech alone — it can be intermittent, unreliable, insufficient, or physically tiring.
  • Visuals are more permanent than speech — they stay in view so students can process the language at their own pace.
  • AAC supports both understanding and expression.
  • Even students who speak benefit — AAC helps teach vocabulary and social communication skills.
  • AAC supports speech development. It does not replace or reduce speech. Everyone who has something to say will always choose the communication method that feels most efficient and accessible to them. If they can speak, they will.

🔄 Total Communication Approach

Instead of relying on just one method, we use all available types of communication to support understanding and expression. This includes:

  • Speech — spoken words when possible
  • Vocalisations — sounds to express feelings or needs
  • Gestures & Signs — pointing, natural body language, key word signing
  • Facial expressions
  • Pictures & Symbols — coreboards, visual schedules, yes/no lanyards
  • Written words — for students who can read and write
  • High-tech AAC — communication apps on tablets or iPads

We respond to all of these as meaningful communication attempts.

🛠️ Key Strategies

👆 Modelling

Point to symbols as you speak — without expecting the student to copy or respond. Consistent modelling builds understanding over time.

🗨️ Parallel Talk

Describe what the student is doing in the moment using symbols. Teaches word meaning and shows two ways to express themselves (speaking and using symbols).

💡 Assigning Meaning

Recognise and respond to all communication attempts (e.g., changes in body language, sounds, gestures) as meaningful. This shows them they can, and do, communicate: what they do and how they respond matters.

⏸️ Active Waiting

Intentionally pause and wait after speaking. Gives students time to process, think, and respond in their own way.

🎯 Following Student's Lead

Change topic, activity, or location to match what the student is interested in. Motivation is critical for communication development.

🚫 Common Myths — Busted

"AAC will make them too lazy to speak"
Myth. AAC supports speech by reducing anxiety and freeing up cognitive resources needed for speaking.
"If they can speak, they don't need AAC"
Myth. AAC builds vocabulary, phrase-making, and social language — it helps students be understood, not just heard.
"Random words on the app means they're not learning"
Myth. Exploring the app is like babbling — assign meaning to it. They are learning that words get reactions and that words mean something.
