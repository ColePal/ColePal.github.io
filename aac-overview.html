What is AAC?

Augmentative and Alternative Communication

Augmentative = adding onto speech  ·  Alternative = instead of speech

AAC includes all the ways students communicate beyond speaking. We use AAC visuals to help students understand us and to express themselves.

🟢

Low-tech

Coreboards, PODD books, visual schedules, yes/no lanyards, gestures and signs, whiteboard

🟡

Mid-tech

Simple devices with recorded messages — e.g. buttons or switches that say "I need a break" or "I want more"

🔵

High-tech

Tablets or iPads with communication apps. Students access vocabulary by touch, buttons, or eye gaze.

🌟

Why do we use AAC?

Supporting communication at every level

✔

Many students cannot rely on speech alone — it can be intermittent, unreliable, insufficient, or physically tiring.

✔

Visuals are more permanent than speech — they stay in view so students can process at their own pace.

✔

AAC supports both understanding (comprehension) and expression.

✔

Even students who can speak benefit — AAC helps build vocabulary, phrases, and social language skills.

✔

AAC supports speech development. It does not replace or reduce speech.

🔄

Total Communication Approach

Using all available types of communication

Instead of relying on just one method, we use all available types of communication to support understanding and expression. We respond to all of them as meaningful communication attempts.

🗣️ Speech 🔊 Vocalisations 👋 Gestures & Signs 😊 Facial expressions 🗂️ Pictures & Symbols ✏️ Written words 📱 High-tech AAC
📶

Communication Stages

Based on the CASS Communication Continuum

🌱 Emergent Communicators

Use body language, facial expressions, and vocalisations. Adults interpret and model language verbally and visually.

🤝 Social Partners

Intentional but inconsistent communicators. Use gestures or a small range of words/symbols. Highly motivated by favourite activities.

🗣️ Language Partners

Use 3–100 words/symbols. Beginning to combine words into short phrases. Need adult support to model questions, greetings, and feelings.

💬 Conversational Partners

Independent communicators using 100+ words in sentences across contexts. Can communicate with familiar and unfamiliar people.

🛠️

Key Strategies

Practical tools for every interaction

👆 Modelling

Point to symbols as you speak — without expecting the student to copy or respond. Consistent modelling builds understanding over time.

🗨️ Parallel Talk

Describe what the student is doing in real time using symbols. Teaches word meaning and shows two ways to express themselves.

💡 Assigning Meaning

Recognise and respond to all communication attempts — body language, sounds, gestures — as meaningful. Follow the student's focus.

⏸️ Active Waiting

Intentionally pause and wait after speaking. Gives students time to process, think, and respond in their own way.

🎯 Following Student's Lead

Change topic, activity, or location to match what the student is interested in. Motivation is critical for communication development.

🚫

Common Myths — Busted

Setting the record straight

"AAC will make them too lazy to speak"
AAC supports speech by reducing anxiety and freeing up cognitive resources needed for speaking.
"If they can speak, they don't need AAC"
AAC builds vocabulary, phrase-making, and social language — it helps students be understood, not just heard.
"Random words on the app means they're not learning"
Exploring the app is like babbling — assign meaning to it. They are learning that words get reactions and that words mean something.
